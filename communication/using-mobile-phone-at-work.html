Using Mobile Phones at Work

Your phone is the most useful tool on your desk — and the most dangerous one. This lesson gives you the language to argue both sides: the pros, the cons, and how to disagree politely in a real conversation. Along the way you'll master HAVE GOT, the structure British speakers use every single day.

Mobile phones have become an indispensable tool in the modern workplace. On one hand, they offer significant PROS.

Using mobile devices responsibly boosts productivity and efficiency. Employees can quickly communicate with team members, check work emails, and access essential work-related applications. Mobile phones enhance flexibility, which is especially beneficial for remote staff, field operations, and hybrid workers. Businesses can also leverage this by encouraging employees to use project management and document-sharing apps on their personal devices. Furthermore, allowing access to personal phones enables staff to handle urgent family matters without disrupting the working environment, ultimately increasing employee satisfaction and trust.

(Điện thoại di động đã trở thành công cụ không thể thiếu tại nơi làm việc hiện đại. Một mặt, chúng mang lại nhiều ưu điểm đáng kể: tăng năng suất và hiệu quả, tăng tính linh hoạt cho nhân viên làm từ xa, ngoài thực địa hoặc hybrid, và giúp nhân viên xử lý việc cá nhân khẩn cấp — từ đó nâng cao sự hài lòng và niềm tin.)

On the other hand, there are notable CONS.

Misusing smartphones — such as excessive personal texting, calling, or scrolling through social media during working hours — leads to severe distraction and dropped productivity. Additionally, security and privacy concerns arise when corporate data is accessed on personal devices, increasing the risks of data leaks or mixing private and professional data. Poor etiquette, like answering calls in meetings or open workspaces, shows a lack of respect and disrupts colleagues. Finally, companies face management challenges, requiring strict protocols like strong passwords, remote data-wiping features, and continuous awareness training.

(Mặt khác, cũng có những nhược điểm rõ rệt: lạm dụng điện thoại gây xao nhãng và giảm năng suất; rủi ro bảo mật và quyền riêng tư khi truy cập dữ liệu công ty trên máy cá nhân; văn hóa sử dụng kém như nghe điện thoại trong họp; và thách thức quản lý đòi hỏi quy định nghiêm ngặt.)

The good side

On the one hand, the biggest benefit is that phones boost productivity and efficiency. They enhance flexibility, which really helps remote staff, field operations and hybrid workers. I can handle urgent matters on the go — last week a client emailed at lunchtime and I replied before I got back to my desk. My company also lets us use project management apps on our own devices, which streamlines communication a lot. (Một mặt, lợi ích lớn nhất là điện thoại giúp tăng năng suất và hiệu quả, tăng tính linh hoạt cho nhân viên làm từ xa, ngoài thực địa và hybrid. Tôi có thể xử lý việc khẩn cấp khi đang di chuyển.)

The bad side

On the other hand, the main drawback is constant distraction. Scrolling through social media during working hours causes dropped productivity, and there are real security and privacy concerns — data leaks happen when work files sit on a personal phone. And poor etiquette is everywhere: answering a call in a meeting or an open workspace is just rude. It also blurs the line between work and personal life, which can lead to digital burnout. Part 3Grammar Focus

HAVE GOT

The structure British speakers use every day for possessions, schedules and illnesses. Same meaning as have — different shape.

The Big Idea

"Have got" = "have"

📱 Think of it like a phone and a phone case. I have a new phone and I've got a new phone mean exactly the same thing — the phone is identical. Got is just the case: it changes how it looks and feels, not what it does.

British speakers snap the case on almost every time they speak. Americans usually leave it off. In writing, everyone tends to leave it off — it's slightly more formal that way.
⚠️ The three traps: ① Past tense has NO got → I had a bike, never I had got a bike. ② Have got is only for state, not actions → I have breakfast at 7:30, never I have got breakfast. ③ Questions and negatives never use do/does with got → Have you got a minute?, not Do you have got…

✅ Advantages

  • Access to work tools: Trello, Google Drive, Notion, and instant information lookup

❌ Disadvantages

  • Distraction & decreased productivity: social media notifications break your flow state
  • Blurring work-life boundaries: the always-on culture causes stress and burnout
