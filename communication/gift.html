B. Useful Phrases

Stative verbs + gerund / to-infinitive for talking about gift preferences.

GRAMMAR NOTE

Stative verbs: love, like, prefer, hate, want, need

These verbs describe feelings, not actions. Use them with -ing (gerund) or to + verb (to-infinitive). want, need, love to, like to, prefer to, hate to often go with to + verb.
✅ Correct

I want a new laptop. (simple form is fine)

❌ Common mistake

I am wanting to buy a new laptop for my sister's birthday. (stative verbs are not used in continuous form)

C. Pronunciation Focus

Connected speech — linking, weak forms, and reductions for natural, fluent English.

To sound natural and fluent, focus on four core features: Reductions (want to → wanna), Weak Forms (to, for, a, the, her), Linking (buy a, choose a), and H-dropping (wear her, help her).

Rule 2 — Use Weak Forms

Function words are usually unstressed and reduce to a weak vowel /ə/ or /ər/. Content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) are stressed.

Part 2 — Speaking

Talk about your feelings when you receive a gift.

1. Talking About Personal Feelings

💡 Tip: "Feel" is normally a stative verb, but in everyday spoken English it can also be used in the continuous form to describe a feeling happening right now — the basic meaning doesn't change.

Formula 1

S + am/is/are + feeling + adjective

e.g. I am feeling amused when my mom gives me 5 million on my birthday.

Formula 2

S + am/is/are + adjective

e.g. I am amused when my mom gives me 5 million on my birthday.

2. Adverbs of Intensity

Formula

S + am/is/are + [adverb] + adjective

e.g. I am very amused when my mom gives me 5 million on my birthday.
